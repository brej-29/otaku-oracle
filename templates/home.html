What is “Otaku”?

Otaku began as a polite form of address in Japanese and evolved in the 1980s to label passionate anime/manga fans. Today its tone varies by context—neutral to affectionate in many communities, sometimes pejorative in others.

Anime 101

Japanese animation—hand-drawn and CGI—spanning TV, film, and OVA/ONA across every genre from shōnen action to quiet slice-of-life.

Manga 101

Japanese comics/graphic novels, typically black-and-white and serialized in magazines, then collected as tankōbon volumes—often adapted into anime.

Mini Timeline

  • 1970s–Early 1980s Fan circles explode; Comiket (1975) launches dōjin culture. “Otaku” begins as an honorific form of “you,” then spreads in fan speech.
  • 1983–1992 Critic Akio Nakamori popularizes “otaku-zoku” (1983) pejoratively. The 1989 Tsutomu Miyazaki case fuels a moral panic; media stigmatizes fans. Akihabara starts shifting from electronics to anime/PC subculture.
  • 1990s Overseas fandom surges: Anime Expo ’92, Otakon ’94. Fansubs via VHS/LD and early IRC groups spread titles like Sailor Moon and Neon Genesis Evangelion. Dōjin/figures grow; Comiket crowds soar.
  • 2000s Broadband, BitTorrent, scanlations; “moe” trend. Streaming era begins: Crunchyroll (2006 → legal by 2009), Funimation’s online streaming (2009). Nico Nico Douga (2006) supercharges remix culture.
  • 2010s Simulcasts/simuldubs become standard; global hits like Attack on Titan and Demon Slayer. Japan’s Cool Japan initiatives promote pop culture. Mobile/gacha games (FGO) entrench “otaku” consumption worldwide.
  • 2020s–Now VTubers (Hololive/Nijisanji) and livestream culture boom; theatrical successes (e.g., Mugen Train, 2020). Streaming consolidation (Funimation↔Crunchyroll, 2022). “Otaku” is widely reclaimed by fans, though tone still depends on context.
