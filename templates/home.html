What is “Otaku”?

Otaku began as a polite form of address in Japanese and evolved in the 1980s to label passionate anime/manga fans. Today its tone varies by context—neutral to affectionate in many communities, sometimes pejorative in others.

Anime 101

Japanese animation—hand-drawn and CGI—spanning TV, film, and OVA/ONA across every genre from shōnen action to quiet slice-of-life.

Manga 101

Japanese comics/graphic novels, typically black-and-white and serialized in magazines, then collected as tankōbon volumes—often adapted into anime.

Mini Timeline

  • 1970s–Early ’80s Fanzines go brrr → Comiket (’75) spawns dōjin mayhem. “Otaku” slang spreads in fan circles; mecha/space-opera era ignites.
  • 1983–1992 “Otaku” hits mainstream via critic Akio Nakamori; late-’80s media panic. Akihabara begins its glow-up from parts to pop-culture HQ.
  • 1990s Fansub tapes + IRC couriers; con boom (AX ’92, Otakon ’94). Sailor Moon & Evangelion turn casuals into lifers.
  • 2000s Broadband, scanlations, AMVs, garage kits. Nico Nico and Vocaloid culture explode. Crunchyroll goes legit; simulcasts start to appear.
  • 2010s Simulcasts/simuldubs become default; global hits like Attack on Titan & Demon Slayer. Isekai & idol waves; mobile gacha claims our wallets.
  • 2020s–Now VTubers go mainstream; theatrical smashes (Mugen Train). Streaming consolidates, anime is everywhere, and “otaku” is proudly self-owned.
